About

There are currently thousands of professional tennis players around the world; however, only the top 150 or so ranked players play in ATP or WTA events on a regular basis. The ATP and WTA are the organizations for men and women tennis players, respectively, that plan, organize, and govern the pro game and the tournaments in which the professional players participate. The players themselves are independent contractors, but they are subject to the rules and contractual obligations set by these organizations.

Both ATP and WTA tournaments are assigned various levels determined by the maximum number of points available to the tournament's winner. These levels are:

  • Majors: These are the 4 major, or "grand slam", events held each year. Each major tournament lasts 14 or 15 days. The winner of each major earns 2000 ranking points.
  • 1000s(WTA) or Masters(ATP): As the WTA designation signifies, the winner of each of these tournaments earns 1000 ranking points. There are usually around 2 of these tournaments played back-to-back during each swing of the season — that is, Middle-Eastern hard court, American Spring hard court, European clay, American Summer hard court, and Asian hard court. Tournaments at this level last between 7 and 11 days.
  • 500s: These tournaments usually play at smaller venues and have fewer main-draw participants. However, they often draw many of the higher ranked players on tour.
  • 250s: The lowest level of ATP/WTA 'major-league' events and the smallest venues. These tournaments are great opportunities to see up-and-coming talents try to work their way up the rankings.

There are other professional tours, such as Challenger, ITF, and WTA 125, that put on tournaments at tennis clubs around the world in which players outside the top 150 regularly play. Ascension from these levels into the ATP/WTA tournaments can be difficult, but it can be done. One such player from Canada, Victoria Mboko, began 2025 with a ranking outside the top 300 but is now in the top 10.

Seasons

Over 30 years ago, at a time when I only had access to the 4 major tennis tournaments — the Australian Open, the French Open, The Championships at Wimbledon, and the U.S. Open — I missed the game terribly in the weeks and months that intervened between them. Now, through various media offerings, there are multiple tournaments available for viewing most weeks between mid-January and mid-November.

Tennis is played on three surfaces — hard court, clay, and grass. For the most part, tournaments that share a surface type are grouped into specific portions of the season. Below is a table showing court types, areas of the world, and the months during the season when events are played on that surface:

Time of Year Area of the World Surface
January Australia Hard Court
February Middle East Hard Court
March United States Hard Court
April - June Europe Clay
June - July England Grass
August - September North America Hard Court
October China Hard Court

Year-end finals are played in November. The men's ATP Final is played in Turin, Italy, and the women's WTA Final is in Riyadh, Saudi Arabia. Both are played on indoor hard courts.

Most players will enter 15 to 20 tournaments a year. In addition to this, there are world team tournaments each year with progressive rounds scheduled throughout the season. After a brief 6-week offseason, it all begins again in January.

Sites

Professional tennis players live much of their lives on the road. While sections of the season tend to be grouped in specific areas of the world, each tournament is held in a different city. Players will often vie for a title in the tournament's final round on a Saturday or Sunday, then travel to a new city to begin another event on Monday or Tuesday of the very next week.
